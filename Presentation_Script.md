What the Epstein Case Reveals — Speaker Script

SPEAKER 1 — INTRODUCTION & FRAMING  (≈ 7 minutes, slides 1–6)
==================================================================

[SLIDE 1 — TITLE]
Good morning. Our presentation is titled "Transnational Crime and the Limits of International Justice: What the Epstein Case Reveals." We chose this case not as a true-crime story, but as a lens. It lets us ask a serious question in international and comparative law: what happens when a crime is global, but the institutions meant to punish it are national? Over the next half hour, the four of us will work through that question together.

[SLIDE 2 — OUR CENTRAL QUESTION & ROADMAP]
Here is the question that ties everything together: why is transnational sexual exploitation so hard to prosecute, and what do international law and cross-border cooperation actually need in order to work? To answer it, we've split the talk into four parts. I'll set up the case and why it is international. My colleague will take the legal framework — the treaties and the machinery of cooperation. Our third speaker looks at how wealth and mobility affect accountability. And our fourth speaker turns to transparency, reform, and a comparison with Japan. One ground rule throughout: we center the victims and the legal systems, not sensational detail.

[SLIDE 3 — PART 1 DIVIDER: FRAMING & THE INTERNATIONAL SCOPE]
So let me begin with the framing — who Epstein was, and why this case crosses borders.

[SLIDE 4 — WHO WAS JEFFREY EPSTEIN?]
Jeffrey Epstein was an American financier who built enormous wealth and a network of powerful, international contacts. In 2005 and 2006, police in Palm Beach, Florida investigated him for the abuse of minors. That led, in 2008, to a controversial state-level plea deal, after which he registered as a sex offender. More than a decade later, in 2019, he was arrested on federal sex-trafficking charges in New York — and he died in custody in August of that year, in a death ruled a suicide. Why does he matter for a law presentation? Because his death meant that accountability could no longer run through him. It had to be pursued through other people, and across other countries. The case became a test of how legal systems cooperate.

[SLIDE 5 — A CASE THAT SPANNED TWO DECADES]
It helps to see the timeline. In 2008, the Florida plea deal — widely criticised as far too lenient. In 2019, the federal trafficking charges in New York, and his death in custody. In 2021, his associate Ghislaine Maxwell was convicted of a sex-trafficking conspiracy. And in 2025, a wave of document releases renewed public scrutiny and debate. Notice the arc: across two decades, this moved from a quiet local plea bargain into a global question about justice, wealth, and cross-border accountability.

[SLIDE 6 — WHY THIS IS AN INTERNATIONAL STORY]
And it is genuinely international, on every axis. Recruitment: victims, including minors and foreign nationals, were moved across borders. Finance: the wealth was held through offshore structures that obscured ownership. Property: residences across the United States, the US Virgin Islands, and Paris. Identity: an expired passport carried his photo and a false name, and listed Saudi Arabia as his residence. And a network of associates spanning several countries and high office. Here is the core tension, and it is the heart of our whole talk: the network was global, but criminal enforcement is national. To explain why that gap is so hard to close, I'll hand over to our second speaker on the legal framework.

SPEAKER 2 — THE INTERNATIONAL LEGAL FRAMEWORK  (≈ 8 minutes, slides 7–12)
==================================================================

[SLIDE 7 — PART 2 DIVIDER: THE INTERNATIONAL LEGAL FRAMEWORK]
Thank you. My job is to show how international law actually addresses crime that crosses borders — and where it strains.

[SLIDE 8 — WHAT MAKES A CRIME "TRANSNATIONAL"?]
First, a definition. A crime is "transnational" when it is committed in more than one state — or in a single state, but planned, directed, or felt in another. Trafficking in persons is the paradigm case, because it inherently moves people, money, and evidence across borders. And that is exactly the problem: prosecuting it requires more than one country's police, courts, and laws to act together. Domestic systems were built for domestic crime, so this is where they come under strain. The question, then, is institutional, not just moral: can sovereign systems coordinate fast enough?

[SLIDE 9 — THE PALERMO PROTOCOL (2000)]
International law's main answer begins with the Palermo Protocol of 2000 — its full name is the UN Protocol to Prevent, Suppress and Punish Trafficking in Persons. It gave the world its first agreed definition of trafficking, built on three elements: an act, a means, and the purpose of exploitation. Importantly, where the victim is a child, the "means" element is not required — any recruitment for exploitation qualifies. The Protocol obliges states that join it to criminalise trafficking in their own domestic law. With more than 190 states parties, it gives almost every country a shared vocabulary — and that shared vocabulary is a precondition for cooperation.

[SLIDE 10 — UNTOC & CROSS-BORDER COOPERATION]
The Protocol sits under a parent treaty: the UN Convention against Transnational Organized Crime, or UNTOC. The two operate together. UNTOC provides the framework for the practical tools of cooperation — extradition, mutual legal assistance, and joint investigations. It can even serve as a legal basis for cooperation when two countries have no bilateral treaty between them, and it encourages confiscating criminal assets and protecting victims and witnesses. So, in short: the machinery for international cooperation already exists on paper.

[SLIDE 11 — JURISDICTION, EXTRADITION & MLATs]
The harder question is making that machinery run. Start with jurisdiction: a state can usually claim it based on its territory, or on the nationality of the offender or the victim. That means several states may have a concurrent right to prosecute the very same conduct. To bring a suspect from one country to another, you generally need extradition — which typically requires a treaty plus "dual criminality," meaning the act is an offence in both states. To move evidence, testimony, and records, you use Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties, or MLATs. The friction is real: these requests are slow, they can be refused, and they tend to stall precisely when a suspect is wealthy or well-connected. So the tools exist — but speed and political will decide whether they actually work.

[SLIDE 12 — FOREIGN NATIONALS & QUASI-DIPLOMATIC FIGURES]
This case shows those frictions through its people. Ghislaine Maxwell, a British national, was convicted in 2021 of a sex-trafficking conspiracy in a US court. Prince Andrew settled a US civil claim in 2022 and later stepped back from his royal titles. The lesson is twofold. High status confers no criminal immunity — but it can create real practical and diplomatic hurdles. And foreign nationality complicates arrest, extradition, and the gathering of evidence abroad. A discipline for us as we discuss this: stick to what has been adjudicated — charges, a conviction, a settled civil case — not speculation. With the framework in place, our third speaker takes up wealth and mobility.

SPEAKER 3 — WEALTH, MOBILITY & IMPUNITY  (≈ 7 minutes, slides 13–16)
==================================================================

[SLIDE 13 — PART 3 DIVIDER: WEALTH, MOBILITY & IMPUNITY]
Thanks. I want to look at how resources can delay accountability — though, as we'll see, not always defeat it.

[SLIDE 14 — OFFSHORE WEALTH & GLOBAL MOBILITY]
Consider the mechanics. Offshore structures and trusts can obscure who really owns an asset. Property in several jurisdictions gives a person places to live, to store value, and to move between. Private travel reduces the friction and the scrutiny of ordinary border crossings. And multiple identity documents — including that false-name passport — illustrate the capacity to evade. Put together, these things convert wealth into mobility, and mobility into delay. And I want to stress: none of this is unique to one man. It is how illicit elite wealth tends to behave, anywhere in the world.

[SLIDE 15 — "ELITE IMPUNITY": 2008 vs 2019]
The clearest way to see the effect is to compare two moments. In 2008, in Florida: a state-level plea deal. He served around thirteen months, with work release. A non-prosecution agreement shielded others, and victims said they were not properly consulted. Now 2019, at the federal level in New York: full sex-trafficking charges, held without bail, treated as serious organised wrongdoing — a case that ended only with his death in custody. Same underlying conduct; two very different responses, a decade apart. That contrast is what people mean when they talk about "elite impunity" — not that the law never catches up, but that wealth can buy a softer outcome and a great deal of time.

[SLIDE 16 — WOULD OTHER SYSTEMS HAVE ACTED DIFFERENTLY?]
That raises a comparative-law question. Would other systems have handled this differently? Think about the contrasts. Adversarial versus inquisitorial: France's investigating magistrates can drive a case in a way that differs from US prosecutors. Victim participation: some systems give victims a formal role earlier in the process. Limitation periods: how long after the abuse can charges still be brought? And plea bargaining is less central in many civil-law systems, which changes the incentive to settle a case quietly. So the central question I'll leave for the room is this: would the UK, France, or Japan have acted faster — or slower? Our final speaker takes us to accountability and the lessons.

SPEAKER 4 — ACCOUNTABILITY, TRANSPARENCY & CONCLUSION  (≈ 8 minutes, slides 17–20)
==================================================================

[SLIDE 17 — PART 4 DIVIDER: ACCOUNTABILITY, TRANSPARENCY & LESSONS]
Thank you. I'll close on accountability — and specifically on how we design cross-border justice so that it actually protects victims.

[SLIDE 18 — THE 2025 DOCUMENT-RELEASE WAVE]
In 2025, transparency legislation in the United States drove a large release of case-related documents. The intention was good: to answer a genuine public demand for accountability and openness. But survivors raised a serious concern — that the disclosures made it easy to identify the people who had been abused, yet not the people who may have been involved in the abuse. That is a real tension: between the public's interest in transparency, and the privacy and safety of victims. The lesson is that transparency is not automatically just. Its design — what you release, and what you protect — determines who it actually serves.

[SLIDE 19 — REFORM PROPOSALS & A JAPAN COMPARISON]
So what would better look like? Four practical reforms. First, faster and better-resourced MLAT and extradition channels. Second, victim-centred disclosure rules that shield victims' identities by default. Third, stronger cross-border tracing and recovery of criminal assets. And fourth, harmonised definitions, so cooperation isn't blocked just because two countries' laws don't match. For our audience here, the Japan angle is instructive: Japan has a national anti-trafficking action framework and is assessed every year in the US State Department's Trafficking in Persons report — a useful benchmark for how a non-Western system measures up on cross-border cooperation. The encouraging point is that better justice across borders is mostly an engineering problem, not a gap in principle.

[SLIDE 20 — CONCLUSION: FOUR TAKEAWAYS]
Let me pull the whole talk together with four takeaways. One: crime crosses borders, but justice often doesn't — networks are global, while enforcement stays national. Two: the legal tools already exist — Palermo and UNTOC provide the framework, and the gap is coordination, not law. Three: wealth buys time, not immunity — mobility delayed accountability here, but did not finally prevent it. And four: transparency must protect victims — disclosure should expose enablers, not endanger survivors. Our sources are listed on the slide, including the Palermo Protocol, UNTOC, the US court filings, and the State Department's reports. Thank you — we're happy to take questions.
